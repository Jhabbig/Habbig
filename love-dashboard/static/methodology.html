Methodology

How the Love Index is built, where the data comes from, and which decisions are open to argument. Version v3, last revised at the latest commit on this branch.

What we measure

The Love Index is a population-level prevalence-and-quality measure of close human connection — how many people in a country have meaningful relationships, and how good those relationships are. It is not an intensity score (we can't measure how much one couple loves each other), and it is not a values judgement on family structure (cohabitation and marriage count equally as "partnership").

Falsifiable claim: a country scoring 80 should, on average, have lonelier people, fewer stable unions, and lower relationship satisfaction than a country scoring 40. If we can't show that across multiple cuts of the data, the methodology is wrong.

Normalization

  1. Within each subscore, convert each raw indicator to a percentile rank within income tier (World Bank: low / lower-mid / upper-mid / high).
  2. Average the indicators within a subscore (equal weight) → subscore percentile (0–100).
  3. Composite Love Index = weighted average of subscore percentiles using the table above.

We do not z-score then min-max. Z-scoring reshapes skewed distributions, and min-max means the worst country always sits at 0 even if the global state improves. Percentile rank within income tier is harder to game and easier to explain ("compared to peers at similar income").

Missing-data policy

  • Subscore present: at least one of its indicators must be measured. If only one of two, the subscore is shown with a "low-confidence" flag.
  • Country ranked: at least two of the three Tier-A/B subscores must be present. The Activity subscore alone is never enough.
  • No imputation of missing values. Missing subscores drop out of the weighted average and the remaining weights renormalize.

Sensitivity analysis

Every ranked country is re-scored under 13 weight perturbations (each subscore weight ±10pp plus leave-one-out). The rank range across all perturbations becomes a stability label — high (range ≤ 3), medium (≤ 10), low (> 10) — visible in the country drill-down and as a dot in the rankings table.

Contestable decisions

The methodology team has explicitly called out five decisions that could reasonably go the other way. Push back via GitHub issue if you disagree.

  1. Activity at 10%, badged "indicative only". Alt: drop it from the index entirely.
  2. Partnership rate, not marriage rate. Alt: marriage rate alone is cleaner Tier-A coverage.
  3. Income-tier normalization, not continent. Alt: continent is more recognizable to readers.
  4. Drop births-outside-marriage entirely. Alt: keep as a stability proxy in countries with clear cultural meaning.
  5. Cap partnership rate at 80th percentile. Alt: don't cap — high rates are good.

Insight rules

11 small rules scan each snapshot. Each insight is provenance-tagged so you can click through to the inputs.

  • peer_leader — top of income tier, margin ≥ 3 pts
  • outlier — subscore > 20 pts above tier mean (z-score reported)
  • divergence — Partnership × Stability gap ≥ 25 pts
  • triple_threat — all 3 Tier-A/B subscores ≥ 90
  • weakness_flag — composite ≥ 75 but a subscore ≤ 20
  • cap_impact — Partnership cap reduced score by ≥ 2pp
  • closest_peer — cross-tier/region lookalike within 12 subscore-pts
  • coverage_gap — high-income country missing a Tier-A subscore
  • mover — composite shifted ≥ 5 pts since a baseline ≥ 30 days old
  • trend_reversal — 2-leg trend then opposite-direction flip across ~90 days
  • event_overlay — composite move ≥ 4pp across a registered event date (correlation, not causation)

Reproducibility

Everything is open. The composite math has 40 offline assertions covering the percentile-rank, missing-data, custom-weight, insight-rule, and sensitivity-engine logic. Run them via python3 test_methodology.py in the love-dashboard/ directory.

The data pipeline is documented in the API reference. Historical backfill is a single command: python3 backfill.py --start-year 2010.
