Voter Pulse — methodology

← back to dashboard

Voter Pulse blends macro indicators, survey sentiment, polling aggregates, and prediction-market prices into a single read on how voters feel and how their lives are going. The numbers and sources behind every panel are listed here.

Data sources

FRED economic series

All FRED series are pulled from the public CSV endpoint (https://fred.stlouisfed.org/graph/fredgraph.csv?id=…) — no API key. Cached for 12 hours, since these series update at most monthly.

Polling aggregate

Approval and generic-ballot trend series come from the archived FiveThirtyEight poll-data CSVs at projects.fivethirtyeight.com/polls/data/. We aggregate every individual poll's approve / disapprove (or dem / rep) reading into a monthly unweighted mean across pollsters. Cached 6 hours.

Pollster-weighting and recency-decay are deliberately omitted — voter-mood at monthly resolution is the goal, not a horse-race forecast. Override the source by setting VOTER_PULSE_APPROVAL_CSV / VOTER_PULSE_GENERIC_BALLOT_CSV.

Prediction markets

Polymarket Gamma API (https://gamma-api.polymarket.com/markets), scanned across tags us-current-affairs, politics, us-politics, elections, approval, economy. Markets are bucketed by rule (approval, right_track, recession, inflation, unemployment, election) and sorted by 24-hour volume. Cached 5 minutes.

The mood index

A composite of three sub-scores, each rescaled to [0, 100] where higher = better. Equal-weight mean of the available sub-scores.

Pocketbook

CPI YoY 0% → 100, 4% → 50, 8%+ → 0
Food CPI YoY 0% → 100, 5% → 50, 10%+ → 0
Gas $2.50 → 100, $4.00 → 50, $5.50+ → 0
30-yr mortgage 3% → 100, 6% → 50, 9%+ → 0

Jobs

UNRATE 3% → 100, 6% → 50, 9%+ → 0
Real-wage YoY −2% → 0, 0 → 50, +2%+ → 100

Sentiment

UMCSENT 50 → 0, 110 → 100 (linear rescale on long-run range)

The misery index (UNRATE + CPI YoY) is reported alongside the composite as a backwards-looking sanity check; the inflation-expectations gap (UMich 1-year MICH minus realised CPI YoY) captures the difference between fear and reality.

The four-year delta

Every indicator card carries an explicit four-year change. We pick the closest observation on-or-before latest_date − 4 years — this avoids faking precision on weekly/quarterly series with mismatched calendars.

The "by administration" panel

For a fixed set of indicators (CPI, UNRATE, UMCSENT, 30-yr mortgage, gas) we compute the simple mean across all observations in each presidential term. Inauguration dates are hard-coded (analysis/eras.py); we include eras from 1947 onward. Eras with no overlapping observations show no data.

State-level mood (the "Across America" map)

Each state's tile shows its current seasonally-adjusted unemployment rate from BLS LAUS, fetched via FRED as {ST}UR (e.g. CAUR, TXUR). Colour is a linear interpolation from green at 3% to red at 9%+ — the same band the national pocketbook sub-score uses, so a state tile and the national gauge are visually comparable. Tiles for states with no observation show grey.

The tile-grid is a "tilegram" — each state gets one equal-size tile arranged roughly geographically. It deliberately distorts area so Rhode Island and Texas read at the same weight. For population-weighted comparisons use the rankings panel rather than the map.

The election backtest

For every month since January 1978 we rebuild the mood index using only data available on-or-before that month — no peeking at later revisions — and join the resulting time series to every US presidential election. For each election we read the mood at 12, 6, 3, and 1 months before election day and score it two ways: (a) does mood ≥ 50 correctly predict the incumbent party's electoral-college outcome? (b) what is the Pearson correlation between mood at that horizon and the incumbent party's popular vote share?

We deliberately reuse the production sub-score bands — no retuning against election outcomes. The point is to ask whether the mood index as shipped would have called past elections, not whether we could fit a composite that does. 2000 and 2016 are flagged because the incumbent party won the popular vote but lost the electoral college; we score against the EC outcome and let readers downgrade the "call" themselves if they disagree.

What this dashboard is not

  • It is not a forecasting model. Sub-score band cutoffs were chosen from how voters tend to react, not fit to historical outcomes.
  • It is not partisan. Era stats are observational, not causal — presidents inherit, and contribute to, the conditions of their term.
  • It is not a polling aggregator competing with Cook / RealClearPolitics. We aggregate one freely-available archive to give the dashboard a polling signal; for production polling work, use a dedicated tool.

Suggestions for additional series, sources, or sub-score bands are welcome. The dashboard is intentionally legible — every number on the page maps to a public CSV, an open-data API, or a transparent rule.
