Methodology & backtests

Every model the dashboard publishes, with the formula, the source, and what happens when you put it head-to-head with reality ← back to dashboard

This page exists because the value of a number is its method. Every indicator on the main page is computed in plain code from public data; the formulas are here, and where the model is predictive the backtest is here too.

The dashboard does not hide a low R² behind a confident headline. If the model is uncertain, the page says so.

Voter Mood Index

A 0–100 composite of five 0–1 sub-scores, equal-weighted.

mood = 100 × mean(sentiment, jobs, inflation, real_wages, gas)

  sentiment   = percentile(UMCSENT, lookback=240 months)
  jobs        = 1 − percentile(UNRATE, lookback=240)
  inflation   = 1 − percentile(CPI-YoY series, lookback=240)
  real_wages  = sigmoid(real_wage_YoY%)   # ±2% → ≈ 0.12 / 0.88
  gas         = 1 − percentile(GASREGW, lookback=260 weeks ≈ 5y)

Equal weights are an editorial choice. Picking weights rewards people who agree with you. Equal weights make the assumption transparent: every voter feels gas pump pain as much as they feel a soft labour market. Disagree? The five components are in the JSON, re-weight to taste.

Why these five

  • Sentiment: the closest thing to a direct survey of how voters feel.
  • Jobs: "is the labor market hiring?" is the single best predictor of self-reported financial well-being.
  • Inflation: voters discount level inflation; YoY is what they feel.
  • Real wages: nominal wages don't matter if prices outpace them. YoY of hourly earnings minus YoY of CPI.
  • Gas: the most-watched price in America. Behaviorally over-weighted relative to its budget share, so it gets its own slot.

Gas uses a 5-year window because voters care about recent pain at the pump, not the 1990s baseline. The others use 20 years to span at least one full business cycle.

Vibecession Index

The signed gap between how voters feel and how the fundamentals are doing.

gap(t) = sentiment_percentile(t) − fundamentals_percentile(t)

  sentiment_percentile = UMCSENT vs prior 240 months
  fundamentals = mean(
      1 − UNRATE percentile,                       # jobs
      1 − CPI-YoY percentile,                      # inflation
      sigmoid(real-wage YoY)                       # real wages
  )

  gap > +0.10  → voters feel BETTER than fundamentals would suggest
  gap < −0.10  → vibecession (voters feel worse than reality)
  |gap| ≤ 0.10 → vibes and fundamentals align

Each component is computed monthly against its own prior 20-year history. The gap series therefore has a built-in "vs how it usually goes" benchmark — no editorial baseline.

Real wages uses a sigmoid (not a percentile) because the distribution of real-wage YoY centers near zero, which is a meaningful threshold (purchasing power flat). Percentile-ranking would lose that information.

Presidential Approval

Two sources, combined visually:

  • Historical: FiveThirtyEight's president_approval_polls.csv, mirrored on the fivethirtyeight/data GitHub repo. Sample-size-weighted 4-week rolling net approval. Frozen at the 538 shutdown date in mid-2024.
  • Live (market-implied): Polymarket has approval-threshold markets ("Trump approval ≥ X% on EOY Y"). For each end-year with two or more thresholds, we treat the implied prices as a CDF and interpolate to find the threshold where P(approval ≥ X) = 0.5. That is the implied median approval.

The card surfaces the historical line as the headline (with a historical pill if it's stale) and renders the market-implied numbers as forward dots at their event dates.

State-level "Where it hurts"

For each state and DC, FRED publishes a monthly seasonally-adjusted unemployment rate as <STATE>UR (e.g. CAUR, TXUR). For each state we compute the percentile of its current rate within its own prior 20-year distribution. Higher percentile = more stressed.

The 2024 swing-state strip surfaces PA, MI, WI, AZ, GA, NV, NC.

v2 will add state-level CPI for the dozen FRED metro series and EIA state-level retail gas.

Persistent snapshot DB & revision tracking

FRED revises historical data when methodologies change. A backtest using today's revised history would silently look better than what the model would have actually seen at the time.

To keep the dashboard honest we persist every observation on first sight to a SQLite snapshot DB. When a later fetch returns a different value for the same date, we log the delta with a timestamp and surface it through /api/revisions. The methodology backtests can then be re-run against the as-known-then snapshots, not the retroactively-revised history.
