Pump-and-dump schemes: anatomy, red flags, and how to avoid them

This is a defensive education guide — written so the average dashboard user recognises a pump-and-dump in time to walk away. It is not a playbook. Organising or participating in a pump-and-dump is securities fraud / market manipulation in most jurisdictions (US: SEC §10(b)-5 and §9(a)(2); EU: MAR Article 12; UK: FCA Market Conduct rules). It harms retail buyers, who almost always lose, and it can result in criminal prosecution. The SEC, DOJ, and CFTC have all brought charges against pump-and-dump operators in crypto markets.

Bottom line for traders. If somebody is telling you which coin to buy and exactly when, and the same coin starts ripping immediately after — you are not "early." You are the exit liquidity. The people who told you are selling to you. The dashboard's red flags below help you catch this within seconds.

1. What is a pump-and-dump?

A pump-and-dump is a coordinated effort to artificially inflate the price of a thinly-traded asset — typically a low-cap memecoin or obscure token — by orchestrated buying ("pump"), then sell the pre-positioned inventory into the price spike ("dump"). The coordinated buyers profit; everyone who bought during or after the pump holds bags as the price collapses, usually to below the starting price.

In crypto the pattern compresses to a few minutes or hours (vs days/weeks in penny-stock pumps). The 24-hour close-to-close print typically retraces the entire pump and then some, because the pump bought volume without any organic demand to anchor it. Below is the canonical four-phase shape.

PhaseWhat organisers doWhat the chart shows
Accumulation Insiders/devs/organisers quietly buy or pre-mint the supply over hours/days. Often funded via a fresh wallet that just received from an exchange. Sideways tape at low price, occasional 0.1-0.5% bid lifts, no marketing visible.
Marketing & pump Telegram/Discord blast: "Coin X — buy now — target $Y". Often paid CT (crypto Twitter) shillers. Synchronised market-buys to ignite momentum. Vertical move on huge volume; 50-1000% within minutes. Funding flips deeply positive on perps. Order book wallpapered with thin asks getting eaten.
Distribution Organisers sell into retail FOMO buys at every level. New buyers think they're in a "real" trend. Bigger candle wicks, lower-highs after the initial spike, volume tapers but stays elevated. Some bounces designed to flush shorts.
Dump Organisers finish their sells. Marketing stops. Bag-holders panic. Vertical drop on lighter volume; price often retraces past the pre-pump level. Anyone in the last 80% of the move is in the red within hours.

2. Who's involved

Organisers (the apex)

Usually 1-3 people who own a controlling share of the float (or are the dev team). They line up liquidity, timing, and the marketing channel. They typically pre-position large positions before announcing the "signal" to their inner circle.

Inner ring

Paid promoters, allied groups, sometimes liquidity-provider mercenaries. They get the buy signal ~30-300 seconds before the public channel. Often hold the largest positions per-capita after organisers.

Outer ring (paying subscribers)

Telegram/Discord groups that advertise "signals." Members pay $30-$500/mo and believe they're getting early information. They are the second wave of buyers — they push price high enough for the inner ring to begin selling. Inner ring sells into outer-ring buys.

Public retail (the bagholders)

Twitter/X scrollers, screen-watchers, anyone reacting to the "biggest gainer" lists. They buy at the top of the pump or in the bounce after the first dump leg. They are the exit liquidity for the outer ring.

Why it always works on someone. The organisers don't need everyone to fall for it — they need the float to clear at a higher average price than they paid. Even if 80% of the marketed audience ignores the call, the 20% that buys is enough.

3. Where they happen in crypto today

  • Telegram pump groups — Largest "signal" groups have 30k-200k members. Coins announced: usually low-cap (< $50M) on Binance, Bybit, or KuCoin. Public groups announce after the inner ring has bought; "VIP" tiers get advance notice.
  • Discord shilling rooms — Same dynamic, often focused on a single project's community. Memecoin Discords frequently coordinate "let's all market buy at 14:00 UTC."
  • Pump.fun / Solana launchpads — The infrastructure makes launching a token and pre-positioning a supply trivially cheap. Many "trending" tokens on memecoin launchpads are pump-and-dump pre-baked: the dev wallet snipes its own launch and dumps once the bonding curve catches FOMO buys.
  • CT (crypto Twitter) shillers — Paid promotion with affiliate kickbacks from organisers. The "alpha caller" persona is structurally aligned with pump-and-dumps: they get paid more if you buy what they're shilling.
  • YouTube alt-coin channels — Same model, lower-frequency, often coordinated with Twitter shill rounds.

4. Red flags — recognise these in seconds

Off-platform (Telegram / Discord / Twitter)

  • Someone is telling you which coin and when to buy. Especially with a precise time ("we're pumping at 14:00 UTC").
  • The "signal" comes with a price target ("can do 5x today") but no rationale tied to the project's fundamentals or roadmap.
  • A paywalled "VIP tier" gets the call earlier than the public channel. That's not "alpha" — that's a structurally rigged sequence where you are downstream of the people selling to you.
  • Heavy "fear of missing out" framing — "last chance to ape," "moonshot of the week," "lambo time." The urgency exists to override your reflective judgement.

On-chart (what to look for on the dashboard)

  • Volume spike 5x+ the prior 24h average, with the price ripping vertically. If you can't explain the catalyst in one sentence, it's coordinated.
  • Funding rate spikes to extreme positive ("BTCUSDT funding 0.5% for 8h" = ~550% annualised). The longs are overpaying because shorts know it's fake.
  • Cross-venue divergence — coin is up 50% on one exchange and 5% on another. The pump is single-venue because organisers can't simultaneously control five order books.
  • Order-book wallpaper — the dashboard's L2 depth view shows paper-thin asks above the pump's current price. Real demand doesn't have $50k bids supporting a token that just moved $5M of fresh volume.
  • Holder count vs supply — on-chain card shows 95% of supply held by <20 wallets. That's a single coordinated sell button away from -90%.
  • No project fundamentals — the coin has no Github commits, no audited contract, no clear team, just a one-page site and a Telegram. Pump.fun launches are routinely in this category.

Aftermath

  • The chart prints a single tall green candle followed by 6+ tall red candles within hours.
  • Twitter mentions disappear within 24-48 hours. The "alpha caller" goes silent or pivots to the next coin without acknowledging the loss.
  • Discord/Telegram becomes either "we'll bounce, hold strong" cope or sudden moderator-led bans on price talk.

6. The math of why retail loses

Imagine a $5M-mcap coin where:

  • Organisers hold 40% of supply at an average cost of $0.01
  • The Telegram VIP group (200 paid subscribers) buys $20k worth around $0.02 each.
  • The public Telegram (50k subscribers) sees the call and 2,000 of them buy ~$50 each ($100k total) around $0.05.
  • Retail FOMO from the trending list buys another $200k around $0.08-$0.12.

Organisers sell their entire stack into the FOMO buys. At an average sell price of $0.09 against a $0.01 cost, they walk away with roughly $200k profit. Inner ring breaks even or makes small gains by selling near the top. Outer ring is roughly -30% to -50% after the price retraces. Public retail is -70% to -90% by next day's close.

Expected value for the bagholder is sharply negative. Even if you "win" 1 in 3 pumps with a small gain, the median loss on losing trades wipes out the wins. The structure of the trade is rigged against anyone arriving after the inner ring.

7. Legal & reporting

Pump-and-dump in registered securities is straightforwardly illegal in most jurisdictions. Crypto-tokens that meet the Howey test (an investment of money in a common enterprise with a reasonable expectation of profit from the efforts of others) are securities in the US, and the SEC has won most of its enforcement actions on that basis since 2023.

  • SEC tipline (US): sec.gov/tcr. Whistleblower payouts apply.
  • CFTC tipline (US, for derivatives manipulation): cftc.gov/complaint.
  • FCA (UK): fca.org.uk.
  • ESMA / national regulators (EU): each member state has its own NCA; ESMA aggregates.
  • Exchange compliance — Binance, Coinbase, Kraken, Bybit all have abuse-report forms. They have an incentive to investigate because regulators audit them on this.
What to send. The Telegram/Discord screenshot showing the signal, the time of the signal, the on-chain transactions you've identified, and your own loss (if any). Regulators are far more likely to act when they have specific chat evidence + timestamps tied to executions.

8. Decision rules for the dashboard user

  1. Never buy because someone in a chat told you to. Period. The mere fact that the signal exists and reaches you means there's a multi-layer ring above you that's already positioned.
  2. If a coin is up >30% in <30 min, do not buy. Watch the chart, watch the retrace, watch the dump. You will be glad you watched instead of clicked.
  3. Check the per-coin page before clicking buy. Holder count, on-chain card, L2 depth, cross-venue spread. If any look wallpaper-thin or concentrated, walk away.
  4. Pump.fun: treat every coin as a memecoin with token-launch risk. Position size accordingly (<1% of bankroll per memecoin, max). The few that survive (BONK, WIF, POPCAT) are 0.01% of all launches.
  5. Use the dashboard's alerts. Set price-above alerts on coins you suspect are getting pumped, with cooldowns short enough you see the dump in real time. This converts watching into observing rather than reacting.
  6. Don't average down into a dump. The retrace in a pump-and-dump is to below the pre-pump price. There is no "support" on the way down because the support that existed pre-pump was the organisers' own bids, and they've now sold.
The honest path. Pick a small number of coins you understand, hold or DCA into them via the dashboard's DCA simulator, and ignore everything happening in the trending charts. Long-term holders of solid projects survive pumps and dumps by definition — they're not even trying to compete with them.
